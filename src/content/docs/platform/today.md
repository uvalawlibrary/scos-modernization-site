title: What scos.law Does Today

The current public face of the project is **scos.law.virginia.edu**, a
Drupal-based digital archive that has grown substantially since its
2018 launch.

## What a visitor finds today

A scholar arriving at scos.law today can:

- **Browse the catalogued documents** by case, by date, by document
  type, by holding institution
- **Search the metadata** — case titles, parties, counsel, judges —
  for records that have been described
- **View individual documents in a IIIF-compatible viewer**, with the
  underlying high-resolution images served from Edinburgh's Luna
  platform
- **Follow cross-references between procedural papers within the same
  case** — moving from a Petition to its Answers, from a Memorial to
  the Information that built on it
- **Explore curated thematic entry points** — **Slavery and Freedom**,
  **Women and the Law**, and others — designed to bring particular
  strands of the corpus's evidence to non-specialist as well as
  scholarly audiences
- **Cross-reference to canonical citations** for the cases that appear
  in Morison's *Dictionary of Decisions* and other standard reporting
  works, with links to BAILII's Historic Scottish Law Reports

## What it does well

The current platform is particularly strong at:

- **Display of richly catalogued material**: for documents that the
  eight years of expert cataloging have reached, scos.law offers a
  level of contextual richness that few digital archives of comparable
  materials approach
- **Cross-institutional integration of imagery**: the IIIF-based
  approach has made it possible to display UVA's holdings alongside
  Edinburgh's, even though the two institutions' images live on
  different platforms in different countries
- **Case-first navigation**: where the underlying procedural papers
  have been bound to cases, the site honours that work and presents
  the corpus as a navigable archive of litigation rather than as a
  pile of independent papers
- **Curated themes**: the thematic entry points provide on-ramps for
  audiences who might never arrive at the corpus through specialist
  legal-history channels

## What it cannot currently do

The honest list of things the platform cannot do today — and what the
[modernisation programme](/future/) is built to address:

- **Full-text search** across the corpus: most pages are not yet OCR'd,
  and the OCR that exists is not yet tuned for eighteenth-century
  Scots-law typography
- **Search at the corpus scale** that the cross-institutional whole
  represents: the catalog reflects UVA's deeply-described portion of
  the corpus; the much larger body of Edinburgh-held and other-held
  Session Papers is not yet integrated at the level of metadata
- **Find a named individual across documents** without already knowing
  the case the individual appeared in
- **Move from one case to a related case** — repeat parties, repeat
  counsel, related litigation — without manual research
- **Provide a path for catalogue records to live independently of the
  Drupal platform** — a long-term preservation concern that the
  [Canonical Data Layer](/future/canonical-data/) addresses

## A platform in transition

The platform as it stands today is not the platform's final form. It
is one stage in an extended programme of work — one in which the
human cataloging investment is the foundation, the current site is the
public face of that foundation, and the modernisation programme
described in the [Future](/future/) section is what extends what
scos.law can do, and at what scale, while preserving everything the
expert curators have built.
