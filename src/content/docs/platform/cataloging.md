description: The human work that underpins everything else.

The project's single most valuable asset is not a piece of software,
nor a digitisation programme, nor a particular technological capability.
It is **eight years of expert human cataloging work**, principally
carried out at the University of Virginia, that has produced the rich,
structured, deeply contextual metadata which the rest of the platform
displays — and which every algorithmic technique in the
[Future](/scos-modernization-site/future/) section will be evaluated against.

## What the cataloging captures

For a procedurally complex eighteenth-century litigation — say, a
ranking and sale of a Highland estate, with creditors in Edinburgh,
heritors in London, and a defunct trust whose beneficiaries are
scattered across the Atlantic world — the surviving Session Papers
might consist of a Petition, three Memorials by different parties, two
sets of Answers, an Information, and a Decreet. Eight separate printed
papers. Eight separate items in a catalog.

Without expert cataloging, those eight items are eight items. With
expert cataloging, they are **one case** — bound together, ordered
chronologically, attributed to the parties and the counsel who
appeared, located at the court venue where they were heard, and
cross-referenced (where the case is one of the reported decisions in
Morison's *Dictionary of Decisions*) to the canonical citation.

That work, multiplied across approximately **2,400 individually
described case-document records** at UVA, is what scos.law makes
visible today. Of those, roughly **half are explicitly bound to
specific cases** — the substantive, expensive work that distinguishes
a catalog of documents from a navigable archive of litigation.

Approximately **3,500 case records** exist as nodes in the catalog,
each one waiting to receive or already holding the documents that
argued it.

## Why this is the foundation

Three concrete consequences of treating the 2018-onward cataloging as
the project's foundational asset, rather than as work that might one
day be replaced by automation:

1. **It is the ground truth for evaluation.** Every algorithmic
   technique the project will deploy — text recognition,
   document-to-case binding, entity disambiguation, summary
   generation — must be measured against the human metadata. Where
   the algorithm agrees, the project's confidence grows. Where it
   disagrees, the human metadata wins and the algorithm needs work.
   This is not a metaphor; it is how the [evaluation harness](/scos-modernization-site/future/text-pipeline/)
   is built.

2. **It is the seed for everything that follows.** A machine learning
   system needs examples. The 2,400 catalog records at UVA — with
   their parties, their counsel, their judges, their dates, their
   document types, their cross-references — are the examples. Without
   them, the same algorithms would either need to be trained on much
   coarser publicly-available data, or would need much larger volumes
   of expensive human review before reaching usable accuracy.

3. **It is citable in its own right.** As part of the
   [Canonical Data Layer](/scos-modernization-site/future/canonical-data/) described in the
   future section, the 2018 human cataloging will be explicitly
   flagged as the project's **gold-standard reference data** and made
   available as a published, citable dataset, independent of any
   particular software platform.

## What it cost, in human terms

Eight years of work by expert curators, sustained across multiple
funding cycles, leveraging a relationship between two universities on
opposite sides of an ocean and across a global pandemic. It is not
small. The fact that it exists at all is a function of institutional
commitment, of a number of individual scholars and curators who took
the project seriously when the case for taking it seriously had to be
made repeatedly to funders and administrators, and of a research
question — what these papers say about Scottish and Atlantic
eighteenth-century law and life — that has remained intellectually
compelling for everyone who has spent any meaningful time inside it.

The modernisation work described in the next section is built to
protect that investment, to make it visible at scales the human work
alone could never reach, and to do so without ever overwriting what
the human curators have done.
