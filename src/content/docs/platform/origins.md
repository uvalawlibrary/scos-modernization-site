The current state of the project — the public site at
**scos.law.virginia.edu**, the years of expert cataloging, the
cross-institutional collaboration with Edinburgh, and the technological
landscape described in the [Future](/scos-modernization-site/future/) section — grew out of a
period of foundational work that began in 2016 at both UVA and the
University of Edinburgh, became formally collaborative in 2017, and
culminated in the launch of scos.law in 2018.

## A two-track origin

The two institutions came at the same problem from different angles.

The **Virginia team**, working with the comparatively small
(in volumes, though still substantial in pages) disbound collection
held at the UVA Law Library, undertook intensive research to first
**identify the cases** in its collections, then **physically reassemble
constituent case documents** that had been bound and rebound out of
order, **digitise them**, and offer them to the public via a
custom-built digital archive complete with **human-extracted metadata
and curated entry points**. Because most cases heard by the Court of
Session were never summarised in published law reports, identifying
the case name for any given Memorial or Information often required
using contextual clues and contemporary marginalia. The Virginia
approach was high-touch, high-precision, and methodologically rigorous.

The **Edinburgh team**, facing approximately **4,500 bound volumes**
across the University of Edinburgh, the Faculty of Advocates, and the
Signet Library, made an unconventional choice to **"digitise first"** —
working with a pilot of 150 volumes — and then to apply machine
learning and IIIF technology to retrospectively interpret, describe,
and re-aggregate documents that had been scattered across institutions
and across bound volumes. The Edinburgh approach was high-throughput,
designed for scale.

By 2017 it had become clear to both teams that the approaches were
complementary, not competing. The Virginia team's rich, human-curated
metadata was exactly what the Edinburgh team's machine-learning
algorithms needed for training. The Edinburgh team's IIIF
infrastructure was what made it possible to virtually re-unite
documents that lived physically on two continents.

The collaboration was formalised in 2017 and has continued, in
different forms and at different intensities, ever since.

## The 2018 launch of scos.law

The Virginia team launched scos.law.virginia.edu in 2018 as a
custom-built Drupal-based digital archive. It is the product of a
deliberate strategy:

- **Case-first organisation**: scos.law is organised around the *case*
  as the primary unit of attention, with procedural papers nested
  within. A Memorial is not an isolated document; it is part of an
  argued cause, and the site is built to make that relationship
  visible.
- **Rich metadata**: every described item carries far more metadata
  than would be expected at this stage in most digitisation projects.
  Parties, counsel, judges, document types, dates, archival
  provenance — all surfaced and searchable.
- **Curated entry points**: alongside item-level browse and search, the
  site offers thematic explorations — including **Scottish Virginia**
  and **Legacies of the Jacobite Rebellion**, as described in the
  Ambuske, Flaherty & Moulds article — that draw out particular
  strands of the corpus's evidence for non-specialist as well as
  scholarly audiences.
- **Cross-institutional image hosting**: the Virginia team's strategy
  has long included sending digital surrogates of the UVA collection
  "home" to the University of Edinburgh's Luna image platform for
  hosting. This increases public access to Virginia's materials in the
  United Kingdom and Europe and contributes to Edinburgh's machine
  learning training corpus.

## Funding and momentum since 2018

A series of grants and fellowships have supported the work since the
2018 launch:

- A 2018 **William Nelson Cromwell Foundation** research fellowship
  enabled UVA to travel to Edinburgh to identify early
  American-related cases across the three Edinburgh libraries and
  earmark them for future digitisation.
- A 2020 **Scottish Library and Information Council** Resource
  Discovery Grant funded the development of Edinburgh's initial
  EDITOR (Enhanced Discovery with Integrated Toolkits and Optical
  Recognition) tool.
- A March 2021 hybrid workshop, originally planned in-person but
  redeveloped for the pandemic, brought together approximately **160
  participants worldwide** to engage with the EDITOR tool and the
  Session Papers more generally.
- Successive smaller awards from the **Omohundro Institute of Early
  American History and Culture**, the **Centre for Culture, Data &
  Society** at the University of Edinburgh, and **UVA's Andrew W.
  Mellon Foundation partnership with the University of North Carolina**
  have continued to advance the cataloging, digitisation, and
  technical infrastructure that the present modernisation work builds
  on.

The 2026 modernisation programme described in the
[Future](/scos-modernization-site/future/) section is the next phase of that continuous
arc of work — drawing on a decade of methodology, partnership, and
expert cataloging.
