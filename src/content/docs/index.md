## The Scottish Court of Session Papers

Between 1710 and 1850, the Court of Session — Scotland's supreme civil
court — heard tens of thousands of cases that shaped Scots law, mercantile
practice across the British Atlantic world, and the lives of the parties
who appeared before it. The procedural papers from those cases —
**Petitions, Memorials, Informations, Answers, and Decreets** — are the
record of how those arguments were made: who appeared, who advised
them, what was contended, what the Lords of Session decided. Across the
holding institutions, the surviving corpus runs to **approximately
250,000 documents and several million pages**.

The University of Virginia Law Library holds the largest collection of
these Session Papers outside the United Kingdom — roughly **3,200 cases**
from the Skene collection, assembled by W. Hamilton Bryson and his
predecessors over the course of decades. The University of Edinburgh
holds the largest single share of the surviving corpus, alongside the
Faculty of Advocates, the Signet Library, and the National Records of
Scotland — roughly **4,500 bound volumes** across the three Edinburgh
libraries.

For most of the corpus's modern history, these papers have been a
specialist resource — accessible to in-person researchers but largely
absent from the searchable, citable, networked scholarly record.

That is changing.

## What this project is

A cross-institutional initiative led by the University of Virginia
School of Law Library, building on nearly a decade of joint work with
the University of Edinburgh, to:

- **Modernise** the digital access infrastructure that scholars use to
  find, read, and cite Session Papers
- **Apply** contemporary AI methods — carefully, with provenance and
  human oversight — to make the printed corpus searchable, the cases
  navigable, and the people findable
- **Preserve** the metadata and digital surrogates as platform-agnostic
  scholarly data, citable in their own right, independent of any
  particular software vendor
- **Partner** with the University of Edinburgh and other holding
  institutions to surface the corpus as the cross-institutional resource
  it has always been
