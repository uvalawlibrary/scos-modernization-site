The Session Papers do not live in any one place. The picture below is
the working understanding of the corpus as the project encounters it
today; it is not authoritative for every shelf at every institution,
and the conversations between the holding institutions are
continuing.

## The major holdings

### University of Virginia School of Law Library

UVA Law holds the **largest collection of Session Papers outside the
United Kingdom**: **58 linear feet** of materials, encompassing
approximately **3,200 cases** and roughly **10,000 individual
bibliographic records**, dating from **1757 to 1834**. The collection
is housed in Special Collections, organized by box and shelfmark.

The collection is known as the **Skene Collection**. Andrew Skene
(1784–1835) was a Scottish advocate who briefly served as Solicitor
General for Scotland under Lord Melbourne. He died unmarried and
without heirs in early 1835 from what the contemporary press
described as a "brain fever," and his library — including his Session
Papers — was sold at auction in Edinburgh on 16 May 1835 by the
auctioneer Charles B. Tait. The advocate **William Craig (Lord
Craig)** appears to have assembled the original nucleus of the
collection in the late eighteenth century; Skene acquired it after
Craig's death in 1813 and added cases from his own legal practice. The
papers eventually passed to the Society of Advocates Library in
Aberdeen, were sold by that library in the second half of the
twentieth century, and were acquired by the UVA Law Library from a
San Francisco rare book dealer in 1987.

Approximately **38,500 page images** from the UVA collection have been
digitized. Approximately **2,400 case-document records** are described
in the project's current Drupal-based catalog, with around **half of
those bound to specific cases** through the human cataloging work
described in the next section.

### The Edinburgh libraries

Three Edinburgh institutions together hold **approximately 4,500 bound
volumes** of Session Papers:

- **The University of Edinburgh** holds a substantial share of the
  Edinburgh corpus, and a pilot digitization initiative has produced
  approximately **155,000 page images** hosted historically on
  Edinburgh's Luna platform. Edinburgh is currently re-platforming its
  digital collections.
- **The Faculty of Advocates** holds substantial materials reflecting
  the Faculty's role at the Scottish bar.
- **The Signet Library** holds materials tied to the Society of Writers
  to the Signet.

UVA and the University of Edinburgh have been working collaboratively
on Session Papers since 2016/2017, including the Edinburgh-developed
EDITOR pilot, a joint 2021 symposium attended by approximately 160
participants worldwide, and a shared hosting arrangement that sent
UVA's digital surrogates to Edinburgh's image platform for further
enrichment. A current thread of academic-developer work at Edinburgh
is exploring visualisations and non-traditional browsing of the corpus
— work that intersects directly with this project's planned tooling.

### Other holding institutions

- The **National Records of Scotland** holds official records and
  supporting materials associated with the Court of Session
- The **Advocates Library** holds extensive materials reflecting the
  Faculty of Advocates' role at the Scottish bar
- The **Signet Library** holds Session Papers tied to the Society of
  Writers to the Signet
- Smaller holdings are scattered across other Scottish, UK, and North
  American institutions

## The cross-institutional whole

No single institution can speak for the corpus. A scholar interested in
a particular case may find the Petition at one institution, the
Memorial at another, and the Decreet at a third. A scholar interested
in the cases involving a particular party, advocate, or judge may need
all of them.

This project's working principle is that **the right unit of scholarly
attention is the corpus, not any one institution's slice of it**. The
infrastructure described in the [Future](/scos-modernization-site/future/) section is designed
so that:

- The corpus appears as a single navigable archive to a scholar using
  scos.law
- Each holding institution retains stewardship of its own materials
- Provenance is explicit at every level — which page image came from
  where, which item lives at which institution, which catalog record
  reflects which curator's work
- Citation works the same way regardless of where the underlying image
  is hosted

## The whole corpus, at a glance

Across all the holding institutions, the surviving Session Papers
amount to **approximately 250,000 documents and several million
pages**, covering nearly every case brought before the Court of Session
from roughly 1710 to 1850. Most of those pages have never been
cataloged, never been digitized, and — until very recently — have
remained inaccessible to search at any meaningful scale.

The figures on this page should be read as **working estimates**, not
audited counts. The reconciliation work described in the rest of this
site continues, and small revisions are expected. The orders of
magnitude are stable.
