## The Court and its papers

The Court of Session is Scotland's supreme civil court. By the eighteenth
century, when the bulk of the surviving Session Papers were produced, it
had developed a procedural practice in which **the substantive arguments
of a case were printed** — not for publication in the modern sense, but
for the convenience of the Lords of Session who had to read them and the
counsel who had to argue them. A single ordinary case might generate
half a dozen distinct printed papers as it progressed:

- A **Petition** or **Bill of Suspension** to open proceedings
- One or more **Memorials** setting out the parties' substantive
  arguments
- **Answers**, **Replies**, and **Duplies** in response
- **Informations** when the cause came to the Inner House for decision
- A **Decreet** recording the Court's judgment

These papers were printed in small runs — sometimes just a handful of
copies — and distributed to the judges and to opposing counsel. They
were not widely circulated then, and they have remained relatively
underused since. But together, they form the most detailed record of
Scottish civil litigation from the period: more granular than the
reported decisions in Morison's *Dictionary*, more candid than the
official records of the Court, and immeasurably richer than the
newspaper coverage of the day.

## What the corpus reveals

Session Papers carry an unusual weight as historical evidence because
they record:

- **How legal arguments were actually constructed** — not how a reporter
  summarised them after the fact, but what counsel chose to put in
  writing to persuade the bench
- **Who the participants were** — by name, by residence, by profession,
  often by relationship to other parties in other cases
- **How money and property moved** through eighteenth-century
  Scotland — tenancies, sasines, dispositions, ranking and sale,
  mercantile disputes spanning the Atlantic
- **Where the cases came from** — disputes between Edinburgh
  merchants and Caribbean planters, Highland heritors and Lowland
  creditors, kirk sessions and dissenting congregations
- **What the law of the time considered debatable** — and how the
  debate was framed

For a historian of eighteenth-century Scotland, the Session Papers are
among the most demanding and rewarding sources available. For a legal
historian, they are foundational. For a scholar interested in the
British Atlantic world, the Scottish enlightenment, or the social
history of property, they remain underused largely because they are
hard to access at scale.

## The corpus is cross-institutional by nature

No single institution holds the complete Session Papers archive. The
surviving copies are scattered across:

- **The University of Virginia School of Law Library** — the largest
  holding outside the UK, anchored by the Skene collection
- **The University of Edinburgh** — the largest single holding overall,
  including substantial portions hosted historically on its Luna image
  platform
- **The National Records of Scotland** — official court records and
  supporting materials
- **The Advocates Library** — extensive holdings closely tied to the
  Faculty of Advocates' role at the bar
- **The Signet Library** — additional holdings from the Society of
  Writers to the Signet
- **Other holding institutions** worldwide, in smaller quantities

A scholar working seriously on the corpus has historically needed to
visit several of these institutions in person. Digital access has been
partial, uneven, and platform-dependent. The cross-institutional nature
of the archive — its strength as a scholarly resource — has been a
practical impediment to its use.
