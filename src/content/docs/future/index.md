The work described in this section is a coordinated programme of
technological and methodological improvements, planned and partly
underway in 2026, designed to make the Scottish Court of Session Papers
**searchable at corpus scale, navigable as cases rather than as loose
documents, durable across platform changes, and accessible to
contemporary research tools** — including the kind of AI-assisted
scholarship that has only become practical in the last two years.

Every piece of the programme builds on the
[platform as it stands today](/scos-modernization-site/platform/) and on the
[eight years of expert cataloging](/scos-modernization-site/platform/cataloging/) that the
present site embodies. **Nothing the curators have done is being
discarded; everything is being amplified.**

## Seven threads

The programme has seven interlocking threads, each described in its
own page below. They are designed to be implemented largely in
parallel, with clear sequencing where dependencies require it.

- [**Preservation**](/scos-modernization-site/future/preservation/) — a dark archive that
  guarantees the corpus survives, regardless of any specific
  institution's hosting decisions
- [**Text pipeline**](/scos-modernization-site/future/text-pipeline/) — making every page of
  every Session Paper searchable, with eighteenth-century print
  handled carefully and twentieth-century pretences set aside
- [**Case binding**](/scos-modernization-site/future/case-binding/) — turning the corpus from a
  collection of independent procedural papers into a navigable archive
  of litigation, automatically where possible and reliably where
  necessary
- [**Canonical data layer**](/scos-modernization-site/future/canonical-data/) — a
  preservation-grade, citable, platform-agnostic representation of the
  corpus's metadata that survives any change in software vendor or
  hosting arrangement
- [**Search**](/scos-modernization-site/future/search/) — word-level search inside the
  documents, with results that highlight the exact location on the
  page where the hit appears
- [**Scholarship**](/scos-modernization-site/future/scholarship/) — programmatic and
  AI-assisted research tools that let a scholar interrogate the corpus
  with the latest generation of language models, with every citation
  grounded in an actual document
- [**Partnership**](/scos-modernization-site/future/partnership/) — the cross-institutional
  future, with the University of Edinburgh and other holding
  institutions, in which the corpus appears as the unified resource it
  always has been

## The underlying commitments

Three values shape every technical choice in the programme.

**The human metadata is the ground truth.** Every algorithmic
technique is evaluated against the eight years of expert cataloging.
Where the machine and the human curator agree, the project's
confidence grows. Where they disagree, the human curator wins. AI
fields are additive — they never overwrite human-curated values.

**Provenance is per field, not per record.** Every value in every
record carries who set it, when, from what source, and at what
confidence. The 2018 cataloging is explicitly flagged as
"gold-standard reference data" so that the dataset of human curation
can be published in its own right, independent of any AI involvement.

**The corpus belongs to the scholarly community, not to a software
platform.** A representation of the corpus's metadata exists in
plain-text files, version-controlled and depositable in research data
repositories with stable identifiers and citations. The operational
website that scholars use is one face of the corpus; the canonical
data layer is the durable form that survives platform changes.

## A note on AI

Several of the threads below depend on contemporary AI — particularly
on the vision-language models that have, in the last two years, become
capable of reading eighteenth-century printed pages with both
geometric accuracy and contextual sensitivity. The project's use of
these models is **deliberate, evaluated, and provenance-tracked**:

- AI is used where it does work humans could not reasonably do at
  scale: full-text recognition across hundreds of thousands of pages;
  fuzzy matching of party names across spelling variants; detection of
  marginalia regions; clustering of documents into provisional cases.
- AI is **not** used to replace human judgement on questions of
  curation, attribution, or research interpretation. Every AI-derived
  field is marked as such, surfaced in the moderation queue, and
  subject to curator review before it is published as part of the
  human-validated catalog.
- The work explicitly draws on the methodological lineage of the
  **UNC On the Books** project, the
  **Grand Rapids Public Museum Glass Slides** project, and the
  **NARA Index Cards** project at UVA — each of which pioneered
  techniques for AI-assisted archival description with appropriate
  human oversight. Much of the methodology and tooling investment is
  coming through UVA's Mellon-funded partnership with the University
  of North Carolina at Chapel Hill.

The result is a programme designed to use the most capable
contemporary technology where it earns its place, to be transparent
about where it does, and to keep the human cataloging investment as
the anchor.
