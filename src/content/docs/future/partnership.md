The Session Papers have been a cross-institutional corpus since they
were printed. They are scattered today across at least seven major
holding institutions on two continents, with substantial holdings at:

- The University of Virginia School of Law Library
- The University of Edinburgh
- The Faculty of Advocates
- The Signet Library
- The National Records of Scotland

…and smaller holdings at additional Scottish, UK, and North American
institutions. The UVA Law Library is in formal partnership with the
**Centre for Research Collections (CRC) at the University of
Edinburgh Main Library** on the modernisation of the Session Papers.
That partnership, formalised in 2017, has been the foundation of every
substantive piece of cross-institutional work in the years since —
from the Edinburgh-led EDITOR pilot to the March 2021 transatlantic
symposium to the present generation of work described elsewhere on
this site.

## Where things stand

The University of Edinburgh has confirmed its intention to retire
its Luna image platform — on which much of the UVA collection has
historically been hosted — and to replace it with an open-source
next-generation digital collections platform. As part of the
transition, Edinburgh will return the UVA-owned material rather than
continuing to host it.

This is, from one angle, a closing chapter in a particular hosting
arrangement that has worked well for nearly a decade. From another
angle, it is the beginning of a new conversation about what the
cross-institutional whole looks like next.

The project's posture, going forward, is that:

- **UVA takes back full operational responsibility for the UVA-owned
  material** — moving the digital surrogates to infrastructure UVA
  controls (see [Preservation](/scos-modernization-site/future/preservation/)), and serving
  them via UVA's image services
- **Edinburgh's next-generation platform is a partner, not a host** —
  with the question of whether and how to federate scos.law with
  Edinburgh's new platform answered by the standards Edinburgh
  chooses (whether its new platform supports the IIIF protocols
  that make federation natural)
- **Other holding institutions are welcomed into the picture** — the
  Faculty of Advocates, the Signet Library, the National Records of
  Scotland — as their own digital collections programmes evolve

## What federation can look like

If Edinburgh's new platform supports IIIF — and most modern
collections platforms now do — then **scos.law can reference
Edinburgh's content directly**. A scholar reading a Memorial held at
Edinburgh, accessed through scos.law's search and case-binding
interface, would see the image served from Edinburgh's platform in
Edinburgh's preferred form. The metadata layer at scos.law would
provide the description, the case linkage, the catalog context;
Edinburgh's platform would provide the pixels.

This is the most lightweight possible federation arrangement. Each
institution retains stewardship of its own materials. Neither has to
host the other's. Cross-institutional discovery is real because the
metadata at one institution can reference the materials at another.

If a particular institution's platform does not yet support these
standards, the federation question can be solved differently — by
mirroring the metadata while leaving the image hosting decision for
later — but the IIIF-federated arrangement is the cleanest target.

## Cross-institutional research

Beyond the operational federation question, the partnership thread
has a substantive research dimension. The University of Edinburgh
has been hosting academic-developer work on **visualisations and
non-traditional browsing** of the Session Papers — work that explores
how a researcher might navigate the corpus through means other than
keyword search and chronological browsing.

That work intersects directly with the
[scholarship thread](/scos-modernization-site/future/scholarship/) at UVA, which is building
the AI-assisted research interface and the underlying semantic and
keyword indexes. There is genuine potential for shared output:

- A unified semantic index that scholars at either institution can
  query
- Visualisation tools developed at Edinburgh that read data produced
  by the pipelines at UVA
- AI tooling at UVA that surfaces, for users at Edinburgh's platform,
  the same kinds of cross-document discoveries it surfaces for users
  at scos.law

The conversation about how all of this fits together is open and
ongoing. It is, however, a conversation between partners with a
decade of shared work behind them and a continuing commitment to the
corpus as the cross-institutional whole it has always been.

## The wider field

The project's view is that the technological landscape being built
for the Session Papers is **applicable to many other historical
print collections** — the original ambition of the joint UVA-Edinburgh
"Open EDITOR" proposal in 2021. The methodologies, the tooling, the
canonical-data-layer approach, the AI-assisted search and research
interface: these are not specific to the Session Papers. They are
specific to *richly contested, partially-catalogued, cross-
institutionally-dispersed historical print corpora*, of which the
Session Papers are one significant example.

Where the project produces tooling and methodology that other
projects can use, it will release that work openly. The lineage of
this approach — visible in the UNC On the Books project, the Grand
Rapids Public Museum Glass Slides project, the NARA Index Cards
project at UVA — is precisely a lineage of open methodology sharing
between cultural-heritage AI projects that benefit from each other's
work. The Session Papers project intends to contribute to that
lineage as it benefits from it.
